Artemis I

The uncrewed mission that proved the Artemis stack could really fly.

Artemis I was NASA's first full mission test of the Space Launch System rocket, Orion spacecraft, and the ground systems needed to launch and recover them. It launched on November 16, 2022 and returned on December 11, 2022 after a deep-space trip around the Moon.

LaunchNov. 16, 2022
Duration25 days
Distance1.4 million miles

Why Artemis I mattered

Artemis I was the first end-to-end systems test for SLS, Orion, and recovery operations, proving the hardware could work together in real flight.

In simple terms, Artemis I was the dress rehearsal for everything that had to work before astronauts could ride on Artemis II. NASA used the mission to check whether Orion could survive launch, operate for weeks in deep space, communicate over long distances, and re-enter Earth's atmosphere at lunar-return speeds.

The mission also mattered politically and technically because Artemis is not built around one dramatic launch. It is a campaign. Artemis I gave NASA the first hard mission data point for that campaign and turned years of design and ground testing into a real flight record.

By coming home safely, Artemis I gave NASA the confidence to move from hardware validation into human operations. That is why Artemis I remains one of the most important missions in the whole Moon-to-Mars roadmap, even though no crew flew on it.
